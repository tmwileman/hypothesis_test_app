Red Hat A/B Results Assessment

The goal of any hypothesis test is to determine whether or not a difference between observations is due to chance (random sampling varibility) or rare enought to be attributed to a factor of interest (statistically significant result). Achieving this goal requires the right type of hypothesis test. Variations combinations of data types, distributions, and variances require particular tests to achieve a reliable result.

Are you testing population mean or proportion

Hypothesis tests can be broken down in to two broad categories; those that test population proportion and those that test population mean.

Proportion Tests

Mean Tests

Tests of proportion examine differences in categorical data. For example, are Red Hat customers more likely to click on a red button or a black button?

Tests of means examine differences in numerical data. For example, do Red Hat customers with a Technical Account Manager (TAM) rate Red Hat higher on a scale of 1-10 than those without a TAM?

How many groups are you testing

The number of groups being compared in a hypothesis test is also determines what type of test will be run. We can test whether one group differs from a known historical mean. We can test the liklihood of observing two sample means within the same population. Finally, we can test if any one of three or more sample means differs from a population mean.

Step 3

Are your measurements dependent or independent

Dependent data consists of paired measurements while independent samples are measurements made on two different subjects. Suppose we were interested in determining if a new product pricing strategy is more effective than the current strategy. If we test the new stragegy on customers that have already been exposed to the current strategy, our data is dependent. However, if we expose one group of customers to only the current strategy and another to only the new strategy, our data is independent.

    Independent data meets the following criteria:
  • Subjects in one group cannot be in a second group
  • Subjects in one group cannot influence subjects in another
  • One group cannot influence another group
