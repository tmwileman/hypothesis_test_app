Hypothesis Testing

Put simply, hypothesis testing indicates if a difference between two samples is true or not. This is done by assessesing the plausibility of two mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive theories (known as the null and alternative hypothesis) to make a conclusions about a population. If the liklihood of observing the difference is rare enough (typically < 5% of the time) we conclude that the two samples are taken from two different populations.

Consider an example in which we want to determine which button results in more clicks.

Experiment Design

Our first task is to design the experiment. This requires us to balance the following parameters:

  • Effect - How large or small of a difference between populations we would like to detect
  • Power - The likelihood of correctly rejecting a null hypothesis
  • Significance - The probability of incorrectly rejecting a null hypothesis

How we balance these three parameters determines our sample size. In an ideal world, we would select a low effect (< 0.1), while also setting our power and significance to fairly stringent values (0.90 and 0.05 respectively). These parameters enable us to determine if there is a small difference between how often the buttons are clicked. However, detecting small differences between populations while maintaining stringent power and significance parameters requires large sample sizes. It is also important to consider that larger sample size typically result in longer experiments. Estimating the approximate time to complete the experiment can be done by dividing the sample size by the expected observations over a given period of time.

For the purpose of our example, we would like to detect a 5% difference between two populations, accept a 10% chance of failing to reject a null hypothesis when we should have and a 5% chance of rejecting the null hypothesis when we should not have. These stipulations result in an experiment with an effect of 5%, a Power of 90% and a Significance of 5% and would require a total of 16,814 participants (8,407 participants for test and control groups). If we plan to expose 500 customers to experiment a day, the test would take us approximately 33 days to complete.

Test Your Data

Generally, a hypothesis test can be broken down into four steps.

  • Identify the Null Hypothesis - The null hypothesis (signified by H0) states that the treatment being analyzed has had no effect on the properties of the population. In our example, the null hypothesis is that changing the button color from red to black doesn't change the click rate.
  • Identify the Alternative Hypothesis - The alternative hypothesis (signified by H1) states that the treatment has had an effect on the population. Importantly, we need to determine whether or not we want to determine the directionality of the difference. If we are only interested in detecting a difference, H1: μ ≠ 0, we conduct a two-tailed hypothesis test. In this case, the alternative hypothesis states that the difference between the two samples does not equal zero. However, if we are intrested in determining directionality, H0: μ > 0 or H0: μ < 0, we conduct a one-tailedhypothesis test. In this case the alternative hypothesis states that the effect of the treatment is greater than or less than zero.
  • Select the correct test - Hypothesis test assume the data being tested represents independent observations, the variance within each group being tested is similar, and the data follows a normal distribution. If data does not meet the variance or normality assumptions a nonparametric test can be used. The flowchart below can be used to help identify what hypothesis test is best for you.
  • Draw a conclusion - All hypothesis test calculate a p-value that estimates the probability of obtaining the effect observed in tested samples. In general, lower p-values represent stronger evidence against the null hypothesis. P-values are compared to significance levels to determine whether your data favor the null or alternative hypothesis. Consider an example in which we design an experiment to test for a 5% significance level and our resulting p-value is .002. Since our p-value is less than our significance level (0.002 < .0.5) we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the effect of the treatment is statistically significant.
